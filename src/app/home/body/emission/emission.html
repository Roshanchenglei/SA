Emission Lines in Astronomy

Emission lines are bright spectral features that appear at specific wavelengths when atoms or molecules emit photons as electrons transition from higher to lower energy levels. These lines stand out against the background continuum and provide a wealth of information about the physical and chemical properties of astronomical objects.

What Causes Emission Lines?

Emission lines arise when an atom, ion, or molecule becomes excited — usually by heating, collisions, or photoionization — and then de-excites by emitting light at discrete wavelengths. This process is common in:

  • H II regions — clouds of ionized hydrogen around young stars
  • Planetary nebulae — shells of gas ejected from dying stars
  • Active Galactic Nuclei (AGN) — energetic cores of galaxies
  • Supernova remnants — expanding clouds of hot gas

Types of Emission Lines

Emission lines are broadly classified based on their origin and appearance:

  • Permitted lines: Arise from allowed electron transitions; typically strong and prominent (e.g., Hydrogen Balmer lines).
  • Forbidden lines: Denoted by square brackets like [O III], these are emitted from transitions that are unlikely under laboratory conditions but occur in low-density astrophysical environments.
  • Semi-forbidden lines: Transitions that are intermediate in strength, often marked with a single bracket (e.g., C III]).
  • Recombination lines: Result from electrons recombining with ions, commonly seen in hydrogen and helium lines.

Information Gained from Emission Lines

Emission lines are powerful diagnostic tools. They allow astronomers to:

  • Determine the chemical composition of gas clouds
  • Measure electron temperature and density
  • Identify the ionization state of elements
  • Estimate star formation rates in galaxies
  • Analyze motion via Doppler shifts (redshift/blueshift)
  • Distinguish between different types of active galaxies

  • C III] (1907 Å, 1909 Å) – Strong in compact star-forming galaxies and high-redshift sources
  • Mg II (2796 Å and 2803 Å): Often used in studies of AGN and quasar outflows in UV spectra.

Applications in Galaxy Classification

Emission line ratios are used to classify galaxies and identify their dominant ionizing sources. One popular method is the BPT diagram (Baldwin, Phillips & Terlevich), which plots [O III]/Hβ versus [N II]/Hα. This allows astronomers to distinguish between:

  • Star-forming galaxies
  • Seyfert galaxies (AGN)
  • LINERs (Low-Ionization Nuclear Emission-line Regions)
  • Composite galaxies (both star formation and AGN activity)

Emission lines are found across the electromagnetic spectrum:

  • Ultraviolet (UV): Lyα, C IV, He II, Mg II – common in quasars and early galaxies
  • Optical: Hα, Hβ, [O III], [N II], [S II] – accessible from ground-based telescopes
  • Infrared (IR): Paschen and Brackett hydrogen lines, [Fe II], H2 – used to study dust-obscured regions
  • X-ray: Highly ionized Fe, O, Ne lines – found in X-ray binaries and AGNs
