International Ultraviolet Explorer (IUE) and MAST

The International Ultraviolet Explorer (IUE) was one of the most successful and long-lived space observatories in the history of astronomy. Launched on January 26, 1978, it was a collaborative mission involving NASA, the European Space Agency (ESA), and the UK’s Science and Engineering Research Council (SERC). The primary objective of the IUE was to explore the universe in the ultraviolet (UV) part of the electromagnetic spectrum, which cannot be observed from Earth due to atmospheric absorption. By placing the telescope in space, scientists gained a clear view of UV light from stars, galaxies, comets, and other celestial bodies.


Fig: IUE Map of Universe

The IUE was equipped with a 45-centimeter Ritchey-Chrétien telescope and two ultraviolet spectrographs, capable of both high and low-resolution spectroscopy. It covered a wavelength range of 1150 to 3200 angstroms, allowing it to capture both far- and near-ultraviolet emissions. Uniquely, the satellite operated in a geosynchronous orbit, enabling nearly constant communication with Earth-based control stations and allowing real-time observation and data transmission. This setup made IUE the first satellite to allow astronomers around the world to observe remotely from control centers in the U.S. and Europe.

Throughout its 18.6-year operational life, the IUE collected more than 100,000 ultraviolet spectra of over 9,000 astronomical objects, far exceeding its original mission expectations. It contributed to groundbreaking discoveries about the composition and behavior of hot stars, quasars, active galactic nuclei (AGN), comets, supernovae, and even planets in our solar system. It played a vital role in studying phenomena such as stellar winds, accretion disks, and the interstellar medium, and it offered key observations of Halley’s Comet during its 1986 visit.

The IUE not only expanded our understanding of the ultraviolet universe but also pioneered new methods in space research. It introduced the concept of remote observing, allowing scientists to schedule and control observations without being physically present at the satellite's location. Its user-friendly design and vast archive of UV spectra laid the foundation for future missions such as the Hubble Space Telescope. Although the IUE was officially shut down in September 1996, its legacy continues to influence ultraviolet astronomy today.

The vast archive of IUE data has been preserved and made publicly accessible through the MAST (Mikulski Archive for Space Telescopes), which is operated by the Space Telescope Science Institute (STScI) in Baltimore, Maryland. MAST serves as the permanent archive for data from many space missions, including IUE, Hubble Space Telescope (HST), GALEX, Kepler, TESS, and others. Within MAST, users can access the full IUE data archive through a web interface that allows scientists and the public to search, view, and download calibrated UV spectra and related data products.

IUE data in MAST includes both low-resolution and high-resolution spectroscopic observations obtained with its two spectrographs (SWP and LWP/LWR cameras). The archive offers tools for visualization, plotting, and downloading data in standard formats (FITS, GIF, ASCII). MAST also provides detailed documentation about IUE instrumentation, calibration procedures, and the structure of the archive, ensuring continued scientific use and educational value.

The integration of IUE into the MAST platform ensures that one of the most influential ultraviolet missions in astronomy continues to support research decades after its operation ended. It also allows for comparative studies with newer missions, enabling astronomers to trace long-term changes in celestial objects. The IUE/MAST legacy represents a significant achievement in making space-based observational data openly available to the global community.

Galaxies and the Types of Galaxies

A galaxy is a vast, gravitationally bound system composed of stars, stellar remnants, interstellar gas and dust, dark matter, and in many cases a central supermassive black hole. The largest galaxies may hold trillions of stars and span over a million light-years across, while the smallest dwarf galaxies may contain only a few million stars and measure a few hundred light-years across. Galaxies are the fundamental building blocks of the universe, organizing themselves into groups, clusters, superclusters, and the cosmic web, shaping the large-scale structure of the cosmos .

1. Historical Context and Classification

The first systematic classification of galaxies was introduced by Edwin Hubble in the 1920s and his 1936 book The Realm of the Nebulae. He organized galaxies by visual morphology into a scheme now known as the “Hubble sequence” or “tuning fork diagram,” grouping them into elliptical, lenticular (S0), spiral (including barred), and irregular types. Subsequent refinements, such as de Vaucouleurs’s extended system, assign numerical Hubble stages (T-values) from −6 (early‑type ellipticals) to +10–11 (late‑type irregulars), enabling a more quantitative analysis of galaxy morphology and structure.

2. Main Types of Galaxies

2.1 Spiral Galaxies (Sa, Sb, Sc and barred SB galaxies)

Spiral galaxies are disk-shaped, rotating systems featuring a central bulge surrounded by winding spiral arms within a flattened disk full of gas, dust, and young stars. The halo encases the disk and bulge, often containing globular clusters and dark matter.

They are subdivided by the tightness of their arms and the size of their central bulge: tightly wound arms and large bulges (Sa), moderately wound (Sb), and loosely wound arms with smaller bulges (Sc). About two-thirds of spiral galaxies exhibit a central bar structure from which their arms emerge, classified as barred spirals (SBa, SBb, SBc).

Spiral galaxies actively form stars, especially in their arms, where gas and dust condense to create stellar nurseries. The arms tend to be populated by young, hot OB stars, making them appear bright and blue. The Milky Way and Andromeda are classic examples of barred spiral galaxies. Spiral galaxies are thought to constitute around 70–77% of all bright galaxies.

2.2 Elliptical Galaxies (E0–E7)

Elliptical galaxies have ellipsoidal (sphere to cigar‑shaped) profiles with smooth, featureless light distributions. Their classification ranges from nearly spherical (E0) to highly elongated shapes (E7), based on axial ratio .

They predominantly contain older, low-mass stars and have minimal interstellar gas or dust, leading to very low or no ongoing star formation. Their light is reddish and they often host large populations of globular clusters. Many ellipticals form from mergers of spirals, which can deplete gas and quench star formation .

Elliptical galaxies vary widely in size—from dwarf ellipticals (dE) to the most massive giant ellipticals (cD). In galaxy clusters they are common, though overall they make up about 10–15% of the galaxy population in regions like the Virgo Supercluster.

2.3 Lenticular (S0) Galaxies

Lenticular galaxies (S0) are transitional forms between ellipticals and spirals. They possess a central bulge and a disk, but lack spiral arms. They contain older stellar populations and little gas, similar to ellipticals, yet have a disk structure like spirals.

The most common interpretation is that lenticulars are evolved spirals that have lost or exhausted their interstellar medium—or spirals transformed via galaxy interactions or harassment in cluster environments . De Vaucouleurs further subdivided S0 into early/late types depending on features like bulge-to-disk ratio and faint arm structure.

2.4 Irregular Galaxies (Irr)

Irregular galaxies lack the symmetric structure of ellipticals or spirals. They appear chaotic: asymmetric, often clumpy, with regions of active star formation and abundant gas and dust.

Many irregulars are dwarf systems, such as the Magellanic Clouds, and are classified as Irr I or Irr II (magellanic or compact types). They are frequently the result of tidal interactions or merging, sometimes transforming from distorted spirals.

Irregular galaxies host a mix of old and young stars. Some of the smallest are the Blue Compact Dwarfs or “Green Pea” and “Blueberry” galaxies—active starburst systems identified in modern surveys like Galaxy Zoo.

3. Special and Extended Galaxy Types

3.1 Dwarf Galaxies

Dwarf galaxies are low-mass systems containing from a few million to a few billion stars—much smaller than Milky Way‐sized systems. They include dwarf ellipticals, dwarf spheroidals (dSph), dwarf irregulars, and compact dwarf types.

Dwarf spheroidals are faint, low‐surface‐brightness neighbors to large galaxies in groups like the Local Group—they contain mostly old stars and almost no gas or dust. These objects are important testbeds for dark matter and galaxy evolution studies.

3.2 Starburst Galaxies

Starburst galaxies experience exceptionally high rates of star formation—far exceeding their long-term average. Their star-formation would deplete available gas in much less time than a rotation period or the age of the universe.

These bursts are often triggered by galaxy interactions or mergers. The compressed gas leads to rapid formation of massive OB stars, H II regions, and frequent supernovae. Nearby examples include M82 and the Antennae galaxies. Some are also classified as Luminous or Ultra-luminous Infrared Galaxies (LIRGs/ULIRGs), where dust absorbs UV light and re-emits it in the infrared.

3.3 Active Galaxies, Seyferts, Quasars, and Blazars

Approximately 10% of galaxies host actively accreting supermassive black holes, producing emission far outshining their stars. These are collectively called active galactic nuclei (AGN), and they can occur in spiral, elliptical, or irregular host galaxies.

Seyfert galaxies are common local AGNs. They look ordinary in visible light, but their nuclei emit strong ultraviolet and X‑ray radiation with characteristic emission lines. They are typically spiral galaxies and constitute about 10% of all galaxies.

Quasars are extremely luminous AGNs seen at large cosmological distances. They can outshine their host galaxies by orders of magnitude and produce powerful jets and radiation across the entire electromagnetic spectrum. Among quasars, when our line of sight aligns with their jets, they appear as blazars: highly variable and strong sources at radio and gamma-ray wavelengths.

4. Galaxy Properties and Evolution

Morphology often correlates with physical properties: spiral galaxies are star-forming and gas-rich; ellipticals and lenticulars are gas-poor with old stellar populations. Galaxy transformation commonly occurs via mergers, interactions, or environmental effects. Spirals may lose gas and become lenticulars or ellipticals; irregulars may form through tidal disruption.

For instance, “red spiral” galaxies discovered by Galaxy Zoo researchers appear to be spirals whose star formation ceased without disrupting their spiral shapes. These may represent transitional phases between blue, star-forming spirals and red ellipticals.

Galaxy evolution spans billions of years. Most galaxies formed within the first billion years after the Big Bang. Over time, interactions and mergers reshaped them, and many starburst events occurred when gas supply was abundant. Today’s elliptical galaxies likely formed from ancient mergers, whereas the modern spiral and irregular galaxies remain in more quiescent or evolving states.

5. Distribution and Demographics

In the nearby universe, bright galaxies are dominated by spirals (~70–80%), followed by ellipticals (~10–15%), and irregulars and lenticulars making up the remainder. Dwarf galaxies are numerically dominant but often too faint to be included in catalog statistics.

Galaxy populations also vary by environment. Ellipticals and lenticulars are common in dense clusters, likely due to frequent interactions and mergers. Spirals and irregulars dominate in lower density, field, or group environments.
